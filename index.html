Understanding Directed Angles

In basic geometry, an angle is simply a static metric of space between two rays. However, in trigonometry, we define a directed angle as a measure of rotation of a ray.

  • ✔ Initial Arm: The starting position of the ray (drawn in subtle deep grey).
  • ✔ Terminal Arm: The ending position of the ray after rotation is complete (highlighted in bright blue).

The direction of rotation determines the sign of the angle:

Counter-Clockwise (CCW)

Generates a positive angle (+).

Clockwise (CW)

Generates a negative angle (-).

What are Coterminal Angles?

Coterminal angles are angles in standard position that share the exact same Terminal Arm. Even though their rotation values differ, their visual final positions are completely identical!

They are related by integral multiples of full rotations:

θA - θB = k × 360° (where k ∈ ℤ)

The Four Quadrants

In standard coordinate systems, the horizontal X-axis and vertical Y-axis divide the plane into 4 infinite quadrants labeled counter-clockwise:

Quadrant I 0° to 90° (+, +) coords
Quadrant II 90° to 180° (-, +) coords
Quadrant III 180° to 270° (-, -) coords
Quadrant IV 270° to 360° (+, -) coords

Quadrantal Angles:

If the terminal side lands exactly on any boundary axis (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°, etc.), it doesn't belong to any quadrant! It is called a Quadrantal Angle.

The Unit Transition

Degrees divide a full circle into 360 arbitrary parts, while Radians measure angle size using the arc length of a unit circle itself!

Since a complete revolution sweeps out an arc length of exactly $2\pi$ times the radius ($r = 1$):

360° = 2π Radians

180° = π Radians

Deg → Rad

Multiply degrees by:

π / 180

Rad → Deg

Multiply radians by:

180 / π

Standard Reference Values:

45° = π/4
60° = π/3
90° = π/2
180° = π
270° = 3π/2

The Arc Length Equation

The relationship between the radius of a circle, the subtended angle, and the length of the arc carved out is given by:

s = r × θ

Radians as a Definition:

Notice what happens when you set the angle θ to exactly 1.00 Radian. The arc length s becomes exactly equal to the radius r! This is the core definition of a radian.

Visual Ruler Guide:

The dotted vertical ruler drawn on the canvas shows a straight representation of the radius segments to illustrate exactly how many radii "fit" into the arc length!
